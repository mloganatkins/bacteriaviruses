Bacteria Life Cycle

Lag Phase

The bacteria do not grow but try to adjust to their environment and metabolize. They produce required substances for division like amino acids and vitamins.

Log Phase

Bacteria multiply quickly when in favorable conditions. Bacteria double in size during the "generation time" and undergo the process of binary fission.

Stationary Phase

This is a phase of decline in bacteria growth. Growth and death rates are equal during this phase. What happens during this phase depends on environmental factors.

Death Phase

Last stage where bacteria lose the ability to reproduce.

Virus Life Cycle

Lytic

A virus enters a cell, makes a copy of itself and causes the cell to burst which releases the virus into the cell.

Attachment

The phage attaches to the surface of the host.

Penatration

The viral DNA enters the host cell through contraction of tail sheath which inject the viral DNA into the cell.

Biosynthesis

Phage DNA replicates and phage proteins are made by taking over the bacterium's metabolism.

Maturation

New, complete phage particles are assembled.

Release

The cell reptures, releasing the newly made phages.

Pathogenic Virus

3 Examples

Measles

Morbillivirus measles

The measles virus causes an infection of the respiratory system.

Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Cough
  • Runny Nose
  • Red Eyes
  • Generalized, maculopapular, erythematous rash

Outcomes

Measles can be a serious in all age groups. However, children younger than 5 years of age and adults older than 20 years of age are more likely to suffer from measles complications.

Treatment

There's no specific treatment for an established measles infection. However, some measures can be taken to protect vulnerable individuals who have been exposed to the virus.

Transmission

Measles is a highly contagious virus that lives in the nose and throat mucus of an infected person. It can spread to others through coughing and sneezing. Also, measles virus can live for up to two hours in an airspace where the infected person coughed or sneezed.

Human parainfluenza

Paramyxoviridae respirovirus

Commonly cause respiratory illnesses in infants and young children. Can also cause more severe illness, such as croup or pneumonia.

Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Runny Nose
  • Cough
  • Bronchitis
  • Pneumonia
  • Wheezing
  • Ear pain
  • Decreased appetite

Outcomes

Lasts 7-10 days in adults, and rarely fatal.

Treatment

  • Stay home and get plenty of rest
  • Drink plenty of fluids
  • Treat aches and fever
  • Take care of your cough
  • Breathe in steam

Transmission

Human Parainfluenza is usually transmitted by contact with a contaminated surface.

Rabies

Rabies lyssavirus

Preventable viral disease of mammals most often transmitted through the bite of a rabid animal.

Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Nausea
  • Anxiety
  • Confusion
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Excessive salivation
  • Hallucinations
  • Insomnia
  • Partial paralysis

Outcomes

The expected outcome of an individual with rabies is usually death if not treated my a medical professional.

Treatment

If medical treatment is effective then the person that is infected is usually safe. Only one or two humans die from rabies per year in the United States.

Transmission

The disease is transmitted through an animal's saliva.

Additional Information

Rabies can only be transmitted through warm blooded animals.

Pathogenic Bacteria

3 Examples

E.coli

Escherichia coli

Bacteria found in the environment, foods, and intestines of people and animals. Can cause severe abdominal cramps, bloody diarrhea and vomiting.

Symptoms

  • Diarrhea (which may range from mild severe)
  • Abdominal cramping
  • Pain or tenderness
  • Nausea and vomiting

Outcomes

People from under 5 and elderly can become very sick from it and die.

Treatment

Antibiotics - Ciprofloxacin and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole

Transmission

Primarily through consumption of contaminated foods, such as raw or undercooked ground meat products, raw milk, and contaminated raw vegetables and sprouts.

Additional Information

In 2007 most beef was recalled because of the ecoli outbreak.

Anthrax

Bacillus anthracis

Humans generally acquire the disease directly or indirectly from infected animals, or occupational exposure to infected or contaminated animal products.

Symptoms

  • Fever and chills
  • Chest Discomfort
  • Shortness of breath
  • Confusion or dizziness
  • Nausea, vomiting, or stomach pains
  • Sweats (often drenching)
  • Extreme tiredness
  • Body aches

Outcomes

They can survive as long as 48 years.

Treatment

Antibiotics are usually used to treat anthrax. Antibiotics that may be prescribed include penicillin, doxycycline, and ciprofloxacin. Inhalation anthrax is treated with a combination of antibiotics such as ciprofloxacin plus another medicine.

Transmission

Primarily through consumption of contaminated foods, such as raw or undercooked ground meat products, raw milk, and contaminated raw vegetables and sprouts.

Additional Information

Rod shaped and non-motile.

Cholerae

Vibrio cholerae

An acute, diarrheal illness caused by infection of the intestine with the toxigenic bacterium.

Symptoms

  • Diarrhea
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Dehydration

Outcomes

They can survive 20 to 30 years max.

Treatment

Rehydration therapy, meaning prompt restoration of lost fluids and salts through rehydration therapy is the primary goal of treatment.

Antibiotic treatment, which reduces fluid requirements and duration of illness, is indicated for severe cases of cholera.

Transmission

Foodborne disease

Additional Information

Some cholerae is not deadly.
