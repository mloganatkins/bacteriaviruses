Bacteria Life Cycle

Lag Phase

The bacteria do not grow but try to adjust to their environment and metabolize. They produce required substances for division like amino acids and vitamins.

Log Phase

Bacteria multiply quickly when in favorable conditions. Bacteria double in size during the "generation time" and undergo the process of binary fission.

Stationary Phase

This is a phase of decline in bacteria growth. Growth and death rates are equal during this phase. What happens during this phase depends on environmental factors.

Death Phase

Last stage where bacteria lose the ability to reproduce.

Virus Life Cycle

Lytic

A virus enters a cell, makes a copy of itself and causes the cell to burst which releases the virus into the cell.

Attachment

The phage attaches to the surface of the host.

Penatration

The viral DNA enters the host cell through contraction of tail sheath which inject the viral DNA into the cell.

Biosynthesis

Phage DNA replicates and phage proteins are made by taking over the bacterium's metabolism.

Maturation

New, complete phage particles are assembled.

Release

The cell reptures, releasing the newly made phages.

Virus Life Cycle

Lysogenic

A virus integrates its DNA into the DNA of the host cell and the viral genetic information replicates along with the host’s cell DNA.

Attachment

The phage attaches to the surface of the host.

Dormant

The phage inserts itself and integrates into bacterial chromosome becoming part of the host.

Conversion

The bacterium replicates its chromosomes but also the phages DNA and passes it on to the new daughter cells (prophage).

Induction

The prophage exist in host chromosome until stressful conditions occur which cause prophage DNA to exit the bacterial chromosome.

Biosynthesis

Phage DNA replicates and phage proteins are made by taking over the bacterium's metabolism.

Maturation

New, complete phage particles are assembled.

Release

The cell reptures, releasing the newly made phages.

Measles

Morbillivirus measles

The measles virus is that causes an infection of the respiratory system. Symptoms include fever, cough, runny nose, red eyes and a generalized, maculopapular,erythematous rash. Measles can be a serious in all age groups. However, children younger than 5 years of age and adults older than 20 years of age are more likely to suffer from measles complications. Common measles complications include ear infections and diarrhea. Some people may suffer from severe complications, such as pneumonia (infection of the lungs) and encephalitis (swelling of the brain). They may need to be hospitalized and could die. As many as one out of every 20 children with measles gets pneumonia, the most common cause of death from measles in young children. About one child out of every 1,000 who get measles will develop encephalitis (swelling of the brain) that can lead to convulsions and can leave the child deaf or with intellectual disability. For every 1,000 children who get measles, one or two will die from it. Measles may cause pregnant woman to give birth prematurely, or have a low-birth-weight baby. There's no specific treatment for an established measles infection. However, some measures can be taken to protect vulnerable individuals who have been exposed to the virus. Fever reducers
